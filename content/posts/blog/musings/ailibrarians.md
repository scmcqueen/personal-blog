# In the AI Age, we are all Librarians

AI is everywhere (and yes I mean Artificial Intelligence, not Avian Influenza). In the Bay Area, it's impossible to avoid. The 101 has AI advertisements scattered across the billboards like goose poop on the Foster City boardwalk. Every day, I work at my Big Tech Job (TM) where I have (mandatory) AI usage and work on AI products (but the integrity of them!). I come home and read about AI - whether it be papers, articles, or a science fiction novel about artificial intelligences (please check out *Ancillary Justice*, *Murderbot*, or *Slow Gods*).

I'm swimming in AI.

As we onboard to all of these new tools at work and I labor to be fully AI-Native (and thus avoid low performance rating or layoff), I find myself writing constantly. One of my colleagues offhandedly mentioned that he was jealous of my liberal arts undergrad degree, as it has given me the practiced ability to *write*, even when I don't want to, even when it is a pain. So much of what we today is writing: creating evaluation methodology for our LLM output, writing down our context for an agent to perform better on a set of tasks, turning the Gemini/Muse/GPT outputs into actionable product insights.

Yes, all of these tasks require writing. Yet the primary skill being exercised is not the tapping of fingers on keys, but rather the *curating and archiving of information*.

**In the AI Age, we are all becoming librarians.** According to the one and only [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Librarian), librarians "provision of access to information, the conduct of research, the creation and management of information systems, the creation, leadership, and evaluation of educational programs, and the provision of instruction on information literacy to users". Let's walk through how all tech workers (software engineers, machine learning engineers, data scientists, research scientists, product managers, and user experience researchers) are becoming context librarians.
